Project Radar methodology

Version 1.0 — July 17, 2026

The Project Radar turns fragmented public evidence into a commercial thesis that can be examined, challenged, monitored, and discarded when the evidence no longer supports it.

Record states

  • Representative analysis demonstrates structure and reasoning. It is not a claim about a live project, tender, award, budget, schedule, or procurement.
  • Verified public record requires an adequate evidence trail, a last-verified date, and a visible distinction between confirmed facts, inference, and unknowns.
  • Internal research never appears publicly until it passes the publication gate.

The SIGNAL° dimensions

Source strength

How direct, current, and authoritative is the evidence? Primary filings, permits, owner documents, and official procurement records generally carry more weight than summaries, reposts, or unattributed claims.

Investment commitment

What demonstrates that the project has moved beyond intent? Relevant evidence can include approved budgets, land control, funding, permits, contracts, equipment orders, and named delivery partners.

Gate or stage

Where is the project in its lifecycle, and which commercial decisions are plausible at that stage? Planning, design, procurement, construction, commissioning, operation, recapitalization, and distress create different buying windows.

Need specificity

How directly does the public evidence support a likely equipment, system, or service category? A need is an inference until a scope, specification, package, tender, order, or award confirms it.

Buyer visibility

Which organization and function is likely to own the decision, and how much of that ownership is public? Named people are not inferred without adequate evidence.

Likely timing

What public milestone defines the next plausible decision window? Timing is expressed as a range and a trigger, not false precision.

Scoring policy

Public composite scores are suppressed on representative samples. A verified record may display dimension-level assessments only when the reader can see the evidence supporting them. Scores prioritize research; they do not guarantee a project, purchase, tender, award, value, schedule, or outcome.

Updates and invalidation

A useful record states the next event to monitor and the evidence that could weaken or invalidate the thesis. Material changes are added to the update history. Corrections identify what changed and why.

Editorial separation

Confirmed evidence, commercial inference, and unknowns remain visibly separate. Sponsors and customers do not receive authority to alter editorial conclusions. Send methodology questions to hello@thecoldreport.com and evidence-backed corrections to corrections@thecoldreport.com.
